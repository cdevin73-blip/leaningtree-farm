Farm life · In memoriam

Bubs — the one who stayed

By Caroline · June 2025

He was born last. He was born smallest. His mother was Mara Jade — our superstar ewe, veteran of triplet births, the most attentive mother this farm has ever seen. And Bubs was her last son from a litter of three boys born in 2021. We named him Tiny, which seemed appropriate at the time.

I don't go out of my way to be friendly with the lambs. It's a practical decision — when you raise animals you may have to sell or let go, it's easier not to get too attached. I knew all three boys would likely be sold. I had no plans to keep any of them.

But Bubs didn't read that memo.

From the very beginning he sought out contact — coming over for pets and scratches, following along, quietly inserting himself into your presence. While his brothers stayed at a more typical lamb distance, Bubs was right there. Always right there. I sold his two brothers. I kept him. He grew, and "Tiny" stopped making sense, so he became Bubs.

"He wasn't loud. He wasn't flashy. He was just there — the steady shape in the pasture. The soft one. The strong one."

Then Mara got pregnant again, and I worried Bubs might cause problems with the new lambs. I made the practical decision. I found him a new home and arranged for him to be picked up. The day before he was supposed to leave, I went out to the field one last time.

He came over and laid down next to me. He wagged his tail while I scratched his head and back. Just like always.

I cancelled the pickup. I broke the rules — Dan had always said the lambs had to be sold or eaten, and here I was keeping one because he laid down in the grass with me and wagged his tail. But that was the moment. That was when he stopped being a lamb I was keeping and became mine. You can't logic your way out of a moment like that.

For four years he was one of the first to greet me when I came down to the pasture. If I stopped moving and sat down, he'd come over and lie down beside me. Always. Every time. He was wethered and had no agenda, no demands — he just wanted to be near you. He was big by then, one of the largest in the flock, but he had the gentlest energy of any animal on this farm.

In June 2025, Bubs slipped his patella out of place. We called the vet immediately — he was clearly in a lot of pain. The vet came out, put it back in, and showed us how to do it ourselves if it happened again. It happened again that same afternoon. We got it back in. It was fine for a day. And then it wasn't.

The vet told us it would likely keep happening. That it was a structural issue, not a one-time injury. And we had to ask ourselves the hardest question: is this a life worth living, if every day brings pain and uncertainty?

It wasn't. Not for Bubs. Not for a sheep who had spent four years being soft and calm and present. He deserved better than that.

We made the decision. We scheduled the vet. And then it was done.

The pasture felt wrong after. It still does, in the way places do when a presence that was always there suddenly isn't.

Epilogue — written for Bubs by Dr. Guy

He was not made of thunder. He was not made of fire. He was made of earth. Solid. Patient. Good.

He walked slower in the days after. His steps more careful. His breath more thoughtful. He never complained. Never hesitated. He simply stood a little closer. Lay down a little sooner.

And when Mara glanced over, he was always there — like a steady stone that made no noise but held the earth together.

Until he wasn't.

The strongest one is not the one who runs first, but the one who stays behind, quietly, to hold the line.

Bubs was not a legend built from battles. He was a legend built from presence. From warmth. From the simple, daily choice to stand beside the ones he loved.

And now that earth has taken him back.

Rest, gentle one. The meadow is yours. The stars remember your name. And the flock will always feel the shape of the space where you stood.

Always. 🐑💚🌿

He was born Tiny. He became Bubs. He was supposed to be sold — twice — and both times something stopped it. Maybe it was just coincidence. But I think he knew he belonged here. He chose us as much as we chose him, and he spent four years proving it every single day.

We loved him. We miss him. And the flock is quieter without him.

— Caroline, June 2025
