Farm life · In memoriam

Beefcake — the cat who came around

By Caroline · December 2017

In early 2015, we adopted three feral cats from Meow Village. We named two of them Barney and Betty, and the third came with the name Beefcake — which was so perfect we kept it.

Betty disappeared as soon as the two-week holding period was up. She had no interest in staying, and we respected that. Barney hung around for a while and he and Beefcake became great friends, though neither of them had any use for us humans beyond the twice-daily feedings. We were simply the people who put down the bowls.

After Barney disappeared, we brought in other feral cats over time. And that's when we started to notice something remarkable about Beefcake. With every new frightened cat that arrived, he would approach them — slowly, patiently, without any agenda — and simply sit with them. Not forcing anything. Just being present.

"We watched Beefcake get closer and closer to her, slowly, until he was just quietly sitting next to her — for comfort, we assume."

One cat in particular, Dionne, was terrified when she arrived. She ran straight to the corner of our shed and pressed herself against the wall. We watched Beefcake notice her. We watched him inch closer — a little at a time, over the course of hours — until he was sitting right beside her. Quietly. Just there. Whatever she needed him to be.

He did the same with the kittens we eventually brought outside to meet him. He walked over slowly, laid down in front of them, and extended his paws toward them in what could only be described as an offering of friendship. They took to him immediately. They all ended up snuggled together on the deck.

But through all of this — the mentoring, the comforting, the gentle diplomacy with every new animal — he still hissed and ran from us. For two full years, we were not his people. We were just the ones who fed him.

And then one evening, about a year before we lost him, something shifted. I saw him outside and reached my hand toward him, not expecting anything. He didn't run. He sniffed my hand. And then he rubbed his face against it.

After that, he didn't leave our side. He followed us around the farm during chores, always wanting pets and scratches. He loved the sheep too — frequently rubbing his face against theirs, as if the farm was simply one large family he had decided to join.

He came around slowly. He came around completely. And then, just before Christmas 2017, he was gone. We found him in the shed. He had been slowing down and we knew his time was near, but it still hit hard. We had no idea how old he was when he arrived. We only know that we had him for three years, and that it wasn't nearly enough.

He was the most remarkable cat I have ever known. Not because he was affectionate with us — though he became that, eventually — but because of how he was with every frightened animal that crossed his path. He saw them in their fear and he met them there, quietly, without judgment, until they felt safe enough to breathe again.

Every cat that came after him was a little less afraid because of him. That's a legacy worth something.

A note to Meow Village: Thank you for letting us adopt Beefcake. He came to us wild and wary and he left us beloved — by us, by the other cats, and apparently by the sheep too. We are so grateful we got to know him. He was an absolute joy, and we all miss him terribly.
— Caroline, December 2017
