May 11, 2026 New arrivals Sheep

Welcome to the world, Clover

This morning, Tarah — one of our registered Barbados Blackbelly ewes — delivered a single ewe lamb. We named her Clover. She arrived with those classic Blackbelly markings: rich brown coat, black legs, and that distinctive badger-striped face that makes this breed so striking. She was on her feet faster than most humans manage on a Sunday morning.

Clover is Tarah's lamb by Bush Creek Shaun, our founding registered ram. She's a single birth, healthy, and already very much aware of her own charm. She'll be available for sale at $350 — contact us if you're interested in adding a little Leaning Tree bloodline to your flock. Tess is due May 24th, so the spring lamb season isn't quite over yet.

April 2026 New arrivals Alpacas

Two new faces, one very full trailer

The alpaca herd at Leaning Tree has a way of growing on you. Slowly at first — a visit here, a daydream there — and then suddenly it's a Saturday morning in April and you're backing a trailer down someone else's driveway thinking, yes, this is fine, this is a completely normal thing that normal people do.

Meet KCAF Kea Aka — two years old, brilliant white, and already giving us a look that suggests she knows exactly how photogenic she is. And KCAF Isla Mae — three years old, light silver grey, with the kind of composed, alert expression that makes you think she's already taken stock of the situation and found it acceptable. Barely.

They join Flirt, Spangle, Luci-Fur, and Arabella — bringing our alpaca herd to six. The existing crew will have opinions about this. They always do. Updates to follow once the dust (and hay) settles.

June 2025 In memoriam Farm life

Bubs — the one who stayed

He was born last. He was born smallest. His mother was Mara Jade — our superstar ewe, veteran of triplet births, the most attentive mother this farm has ever seen. And Bubs was her last son from a litter of three boys born in 2021. We named him Tiny, which seemed appropriate at the time.

I don't go out of my way to be friendly with the lambs. It's a practical decision — when you raise animals you may have to sell or let go, it's easier not to get too attached. But Bubs didn't read that memo. From the very beginning he sought out contact, coming over for pets and scratches, quietly inserting himself into your presence. I sold his brothers. I kept him.

Then Mara got pregnant again, and I made the practical decision to sell him too. The day before he was supposed to leave, I went out to the field one last time. He came over and laid down next to me. He wagged his tail while I scratched his head and back. I cancelled the pickup. He was mine. You can't logic your way out of a moment like that.

December 2017 In memoriam Farm life

Beefcake — the cat who came around

In early 2015, we adopted three feral cats from Meow Village. Beefcake came with his name already — which was so perfect we kept it. For two years he wanted nothing to do with us humans. But he had infinite patience for every frightened animal that crossed his path, sitting quietly beside them until they felt safe enough to breathe again.
